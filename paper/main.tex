\begin{abstract}
Kinematic humanoid motion generators such as MotionBricks can synthesize
diverse Unitree G1 reference trajectories, but these references are not
guaranteed to be dynamically executable. We study a test-time screening method
with no generator or controller retraining: sample $K$ MotionBricks candidates, score each with a
MuJoCo inverse-dynamics heuristic critic, and select the lowest-risk clip. On a
39-identity K sweep, mean heuristic risk decreases from 38.90 at $K=1$ to 15.93
at $K=8$, and heuristic-accepted clips increase from 5/39 to 26/39. A larger K=1/K=8
extension over 105 paired motion identities reproduces the effect: mean risk
decreases from 35.32 to 13.58, heuristic-accepted clips increase from 25/105 to 78/105,
and 81/105 identities improve. A 105-row mode-seed audit over 15 unique local
mode prompts shows K=8 broadly preserves trajectory/style proxies
(mean 0.568 vs 0.583 for K=1),
while a contact-quality audit improves artifact score from 0.274 to 0.248.
The method is strongest on upright locomotion
and expressive walking styles, weaker on crawling, and harmful for already
stable idle clips. Controller experiments are negative controls: neither weak PD
nor torque-limited computed-torque tracking validates per-clip critic scores.
We also run the released SONIC G1 tracking-policy encoder/decoder in an
approximate MuJoCo harness. After fixing the harness, K=8 is roughly neutral
under learned-policy tracking on 105 K=1/K=8 pairs: mean survival changes from
2.005s to 2.054s and RMSE from 0.339 to 0.334 rad, with 98/105 falls in both
groups and 0\% torque saturation. A controller-aware oracle over the two
available candidates improves survival to 2.299s and RMSE to 0.310 rad, while
a four-way oracle over K=1/4/8/16 on the smaller 39-identity sweep improves
survival from 2.134s to 2.833s ($p=1.9{\times}10^{-6}$).
Therefore, the contribution is a transparent inverse-dynamics screening and
ranking study plus evidence that controller-in-the-loop selection or
planner-policy co-training is needed, not a claim of real-robot feasibility.
Because selection and evaluation use related heuristic scores, the reported
gains should be interpreted as successful risk reranking under this critic, not
independent physical validation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

MotionBricks demonstrates real-time kinematic whole-body motion generation for
animation and humanoid robotics, reporting high throughput and broad motion
coverage \cite{motionbricks}. The current public G1 preview used here exposes a
mode/control interface rather than a full arbitrary text endpoint. Its
limitation section also points to a central robotics gap: kinematic planners can
output motions that violate hardware or contact constraints. Recent Unitree G1
pipelines address this by coupling
kinematic motion sources to low-level controllers or physical plausibility
optimization \cite{claw,roboforge}. This project investigates a smaller but
practical intervention: can we reduce a fixed kinematic generator's
inverse-dynamics heuristic risk at test time using an explicit dynamics-derived
ranking signal?

We treat MotionBricks as a sampler. The first candidate uses the default
deterministic generation path. Additional candidates enable stochastic Gumbel
pose-token sampling and offset the reference seed. Each candidate is a qpos
sequence. A MuJoCo inverse-dynamics critic estimates required torques, root
wrench, velocities, accelerations, and jerk under exact replay, then assigns a
heuristic risk score. Best-of-$K$ selection keeps the candidate with lowest
critic demand.

The method is intentionally simple. Its value is not novelty in inverse
dynamics itself, but a measured characterization of inference-time scaling for
humanoid kinematic references, including failures and negative controls.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Kinematic humanoid generation.}
MotionBricks uses a modular latent generative backbone and smart primitives for
real-time motion generation, with a public G1 demo and MotionBricks-powered
motion generation in NVIDIA's GR00T Whole-Body Control stack \cite{motionbricks}.
CLAW similarly highlights that text-to-motion generators produce kinematic
outputs that need physical grounding, and tracks references in MuJoCo with a
low-level controller \cite{claw}.

\paragraph{Physics-based humanoid tracking.}
DeepMimic and AMP established reinforcement-learning approaches for physically
simulated character imitation and stylized control \cite{deepmimic,amp}. PHC
scales humanoid imitation and recovery, including noisy references from video
or language-generated motion \cite{phc}. These works motivate our validation
gap: a true feasibility claim should evaluate references under a strong learned
tracking policy, not only an inverse-dynamics score.

\paragraph{Physical plausibility optimization.}
RoboForge couples text-guided generation and physical plausibility optimization,
using bidirectional optimization between generator and controller
\cite{roboforge}. Our study is complementary and narrower: it does not retrain
the generator or controller, but asks how much quality can be recovered through
candidate selection alone.

\paragraph{Inference-time scaling.}
Best-of-$N$ sampling and verifier-guided selection are standard in language
model reasoning \cite{cobbe,lightman,snell}. We translate this idea to
humanoid reference generation, replacing the reward model with an
inverse-dynamics critic.

\paragraph{Text-motion evaluation.}
Standard text-to-motion benchmarks such as HumanML3D report learned
text-motion retrieval and distributional metrics: R-Precision, matching score,
FID, diversity, and multimodality \cite{humanml3d,t2mgpt}. The public
MotionBricks G1 preview used here exposes a mode/control interface rather than
a full arbitrary text endpoint, so our prompt-following audit uses weaker
task-proxy metrics and should not be compared to HumanML3D R-Precision.

\section{Method}

The robot model is the Unitree G1 MuJoCo model with 36-dimensional qpos:
a floating root plus 29 actuated joints. Given a clip $q_{0:T}$, velocities are
computed with quaternion-safe MuJoCo differencing and accelerations by finite
difference. We call \texttt{mj\_inverse} for each transition.

The final method is not fine-tuning MotionBricks and is not motion retargeting:
it is candidate reranking. We do train neural surrogate critics in side
experiments to imitate the heuristic score, but those models do not update the
MotionBricks generator and are not used as the final selected result because
clip-level neural selection underperforms the heuristic.

The scalar heuristic is
\[
\begin{aligned}
r =&\;35 e(\tau_{95}/\tau_{\max},1.0)
 + 20 e(F^{root}_{95},10000)\\
&+ 15 e(M^{root}_{95},2000)
 + 10 e(\omega_{95},10)\\
&+ 10 e(\alpha_{95},120)
 + 10 e(jerk_{mean},1200),
\end{aligned}
\]
where $e(v,t)=\log(1+\max(v-t,0)/t)$. Action labels are implementation labels,
not safety labels: critic-accept if risk $\leq 25$ and p95 torque/limit
$\leq 1.25$; critic-review if risk $\leq 80$ and torque/limit $\leq 5$ but not
critic-accepted; critic-reject otherwise.

Whole-clip best-of-$K$ generates $K$ complete clips and selects the lowest
online segment-critic score, then evaluates the selected clip with the full
critic. A 10-batch candidate audit found segment/full winner agreement in all
audited batches, but this remains a finite sanity check. The main results
therefore do not constitute an oracle best-of-K upper bound; they measure the
implemented segment-critic selector.

The paired aggregate reduction is 61.55\%, with 81/105 identities improved.

idle. Static regression is expected: stochastic resampling can hurt clips that
are already low demand.

\paragraph{Prompt/control preservation.}

text-to-motion benchmark. It contains 15 unique local mode prompts evaluated
over 7 seeds each. Each row specifies a natural-language description, category,
target speed, target direction, and expected tags. We evaluate speed,
direction, staticness, low-posture behavior, and upper-body expressiveness
proxies.

\paragraph{Contact-quality audit.}
We also evaluate self-contact, non-foot floor contact, foot speed while contact
persists, and a simple support-proxy diagnostic. The contact artifact score
improves from 0.274 at K=1 to 0.248 at K=8. Whole-body crawling improves more
substantially, from 0.654 to 0.489, but remains visibly problematic: non-foot
floor contact is still 34.7\% at K=8. These metrics help explain visible risk
in videos but are not controller success metrics.

\paragraph{Boundary audit summary.}
The main K=8 result lowers critic risk by 61.55\% and raises critic-accept
labels from 25/105 to 78/105. The prompt/control audit is weaker: mean proxy
alignment changes from 0.583 to 0.568, so this is a damage audit rather than an
alignment improvement. The contact audit improves from 0.274 to 0.248 artifact
score, but it is post-hoc and not part of selection. The controller audit is
negative: all computed-torque rollouts fall at frame 15.

\paragraph{Learned-policy audit.}
To test whether lower inverse-dynamics risk transfers to a learned controller,
we build the public SONIC G1 deployment stack and run its released
encoder/decoder ONNX weights in a MuJoCo closed-loop harness. The native binary
loads our exported references and TensorRT-converts the policy stack, but waits
for live Unitree \texttt{LowState} DDS messages; for batch evaluation we
therefore reconstruct the observation path in Python and label it an
approximate harness.

The corrected result is more nuanced than the earlier buggy run. On 105 paired
K=1/K=8 references, 98/105 rollouts fall in each group, while idle references
survive the full 5s window. Mean survival changes from 2.005s for K=1 to
2.054s for inverse-dynamics-selected K=8, and mean joint RMSE changes from
0.339 to 0.334 rad. These differences are not significant
($p=0.159$ for greater survival; $p=0.457$ for lower RMSE), despite the large
drop in inverse-dynamics heuristic risk.

We then use the 39-identity K sweep, which provides K=1/4/8/16 screened
variants, as a limited controller-in-the-loop oracle. Selecting the variant
with longest SONIC survival improves mean survival from 2.134s at K=1 to
2.833s ($p=1.9{\times}10^{-6}$) and lowers RMSE from 0.328 to 0.286 rad
($p=0.019$). The fall count remains 36/39 because idle clips survive in all
settings. This supports the pivot: physical awareness should be measured with
the target tracking policy in the loop, but the current candidate pool and
policy interface are not sufficient for a real-robot execution claim.

\paragraph{Ablations and negative controls.}
Whole-clip K=4 outperforms per-segment K=4 at equal compute on the current
setup. Gumbel sampling is the strongest diversity source, while seed diversity
helps expressive motions. A neural window critic reaches high window-level
agreement with heuristic labels, but neural full-clip selection is worse than
heuristic selection, so it is not used for final results. Retraining a
full-clip critic on all 210 extended clips improves validation Spearman
correlation to 0.747. A larger 3.38M-parameter residual temporal CNN trained
for 800 epochs on 497 unique labeled clips improves correlation to 0.800, still
below the 0.85 target for replacing the heuristic selector.

Most importantly, controller validation is currently negative. This is the main
negative result of the study. The weak PD controller has insignificant per-clip
correlation with risk (Spearman $\rho=-0.09$). A stronger torque-limited
computed-torque tracker also fails uniformly at frame 15 and does not improve
K=8 tracking RMSE. The learned SONIC harness is more relevant: after fixing the
harness, K=8 has a small non-significant survival/RMSE improvement, while a
policy-aware selector improves survival and RMSE significantly. Thus lower
heuristic risk alone is not a validated substitute for closed-loop tracking
outcomes.

\section{Discussion}

Best-of-$K$ works because MotionBricks has meaningful local candidate diversity:
different seeds choose different references, and Gumbel sampling changes the
token trajectory toward a reference. For upright and expressive motions, some
candidates have lower derivative and torque demand while preserving the broad
mode label. For crawling, the candidate set remains high demand under the
current robot model and critic. For idle clips, the correct policy is to skip
resampling when the first candidate is already low risk.

The main limitation is execution. A publishable physical-execution claim needs
a strong learned G1 tracking setup or hardware rollout in which selected
references actually remain upright beyond short horizons and across dynamic
motions. Our SONIC audit is a first step toward that target: the harness runs,
but most non-idle clips still fall within 5s. The best next step is
controller-in-the-loop selection, planner-policy co-training, or preference
tuning: use controller outcomes to shift the generator distribution, rather
than relying only on inverse-dynamics rejection sampling.

The prompt-control and contact audits also clarify scope. The method screens
among local candidates; it does not create new semantic capability, and it does
not guarantee collision-free or dynamically executable motion. Its strongest
current use is as a transparent triage layer before expensive controller
rollout.
